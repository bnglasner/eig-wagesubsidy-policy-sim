# EIG Writing Style Guide

Full reference for writing, editing, and proofreading EIG publications, blog posts, briefs, and research reports. Synthesizes the EIG Writing Style Guide, Smart Brevity Checklist, Hank-Wallace One-Word-per-Meaning rule, and Write Like the News booklet.

---

## 1. Numbers & Percentages

- **Spell out numbers one through nine** in running text. Use numerals for 10 and above.
- **Exception:** Always use numerals when a number begins a statistical comparison, appears in a table, or precedes a unit of measurement (e.g., "3 percent," "5 miles").
- **Never begin a sentence with a numeral.** Rewrite or spell out.
- **Write "percent" in full in body text.** Use the % symbol only in charts, tables, and figures.
- **Decimals:** Use one decimal place for percentages in text (e.g., 4.3 percent). Round more aggressively in headlines and ledes.
- **Millions/billions:** Use numerals + word form (e.g., "$1.2 million," "3.4 billion people"). Do not abbreviate as "M" or "B" in text.
- **Dollar amounts:** "$" before the numeral, no space. Use "dollars" spelled out only when the currency must be clarified (e.g., "Canadian dollars").
- **Ranges:** Use "to" in text ("20 to 30 percent"), en dash in tables and charts ("20–30%").
- **Fractions:** Spell out simple fractions in text ("two-thirds," "one-half"). Use numerals for complex fractions.
- **Ordinals:** Spell out first through ninth; use numerals for 10th and above.
- **Years:** Always numerals. Decade references: "the 1990s" (no apostrophe before the s).

## 2. Punctuation

- **Oxford (serial) comma:** Always use in lists of three or more items (e.g., "wages, employment, and output").
- **Em dash:** Use to set off a strong parenthetical — like this — with no spaces on either side.
- **En dash:** Use in ranges (pages 10–20; years 2010–2022). No spaces.
- **Hyphenation:** Follow AP style. Compound modifiers before a noun are hyphenated ("high-wage workers"); after a verb, they are not ("workers are paid high wages").
- **Footnote/endnote placement:** Footnote markers appear **outside** the closing punctuation (e.g., "the result was significant.¹").
- **Ellipsis:** Three spaced dots with spaces before and after ( … ) when indicating omission in a quote. Do not use ellipsis for stylistic trailing-off.
- **Parentheses:** Use sparingly. If the parenthetical is a complete sentence, put the period inside. Otherwise, outside.
- **Colons and semicolons:** Colon introduces a list or elaboration; the word after a colon is not capitalized unless it begins a complete sentence. Semicolons join closely related independent clauses.

---

## 3. U.S. vs. United States

- **"U.S."** (with periods) as an adjective: "U.S. economy," "U.S. Census Bureau."
- **"United States"** (spelled out) as a noun: "The United States has…"
- Never "US" (no periods) in formal EIG publications.

---

## 4. Capitalization

- **Industries and sectors:** lowercase (manufacturing, retail, technology sector, financial services).
- **Proper names of programs, acts, agencies:** capitalize as officially named (the Inflation Reduction Act, the Census Bureau).
- **Headlines (print):** Title case — capitalize all principal words; lowercase articles, prepositions <5 letters, and coordinating conjunctions unless first word.
- **Headlines (digital/blog):** Sentence case — capitalize only the first word and proper nouns.
- **Figure and table labels:** Sentence case — capitalize only the first word (see Section 8).
- **Regions as proper nouns:** capitalize (the South, the Midwest, the Sun Belt).
- **Directions:** lowercase unless part of a proper regional name (southern states; the South).
- **"federal" and "state":** lowercase unless part of a formal name ("the federal government"; "the Federal Reserve").
- **"Act," "bill," "law":** capitalize when using the full official name; lowercase for informal references ("the stimulus bill").

## 5. Spelling & Word Form

| Word | EIG Standard |
|------|-------------|
| COVID-19 | Always all caps; always with hyphen |
| homeownership | One word (not "home ownership") |
| Sun Belt | Two words, both capitalized |
| policymaker(s) | One word |
| workforce | One word |
| startup | One word (noun/adjective); "start up" as a verb |
| long-term | Hyphenated as modifier; not hyphenated as predicate |
| data | Treated as plural ("data are," "these data show") |
| healthcare | One word in most EIG usage; check context |
| e-commerce | Hyphenated |

- **Contractions:** Never use contractions in formal research or policy documents. ("Don't" → "Do not"; "It's" → "It is.")
- **First person:** Avoid "I" in formal publications. "We" is acceptable for EIG-authored work when referring to the organization.
- **Passive voice:** Minimize. Restructure to active constructions wherever possible.

## 6. Chart and Figure Labeling

- **Prefix:** Every figure must be labeled "Figure N." (e.g., "Figure 1.") — capital F, Arabic numeral, period.
- **Caption/title capitalization:** Sentence case — capitalize only the first word and proper nouns.
  - Correct: "Figure 3. Share of workers earning below the poverty line, 2000–2022"
  - Incorrect: "Figure 3. Share of Workers Earning Below the Poverty Line, 2000–2022"
- **Axis labels:** Sentence case. Keep short; include units in parentheses if needed.
- **Legend labels:** Sentence case; match data series names exactly.
- **Source line:** "Source: [Author/Organization], [Year]." Italicize if possible in the layout tool. Write out source name in full if three words or fewer; otherwise abbreviate after first use.
- **Note line (if used):** "Note: [text]." Appears above the source line. Use for methodological caveats.
- **No figure numbers in interactive/web embeds** unless the surrounding text references them.

## 7. Smart Brevity Principles (Axios-Derived)

Smart Brevity is a framework for making every word earn its place. Apply to all EIG external communications: blog posts, newsletter items, social posts, press releases.

### The 6 Steps

1. **Lead with what's new or most important.** Ask: "What would my audience most want to know?" Put that first. Do not bury the lede.
2. **One-sentence lede.** The opening sentence should be a single, complete thought. No throat-clearing, no "In recent years…"
3. **"Why it matters" sentence.** After the lede, explicitly tell the reader why this finding matters to them.
4. **Use bullets, not paragraphs, for lists.** If you're listing three or more items, break them into bullets.
5. **Bold the first phrase of each bullet.** This allows skimmers to absorb the key points.
6. **Cut ruthlessly.** Every sentence must add new information. Delete sentences that merely restate what was already said.

### Audience-First Framing
- Before writing, identify: Who is the primary audience? What do they already know? What do they need from this piece?
- Frame every data point in terms the audience cares about. "3.2 million workers" is more concrete than "a significant share of the workforce."

### Lead With What's New
- News angle: What has changed? What is the most recent or surprising finding?
- Do not open with background or context unless the audience genuinely cannot understand the finding without it.

## 8. One-Word-per-Meaning Rule (Hank-Wallace)

Avoid **elegant variation** — the habit of using different words for the same concept to avoid repetition. Elegant variation creates ambiguity.

- **Wrong:** "The researchers studied workers, then examined employees, and finally analyzed the labor force…"
- **Right:** "The researchers studied workers…" (use "workers" consistently throughout)

### Application
- Assign one precise term to each concept and use it consistently.
- Do not substitute synonyms for variety. Readers assume different words signal different meanings.
- Exceptions: pronouns ("workers… they") and clearly established shorthand ("the United States… the country").

## 9. Write Like the News (Additional Skills)

From the *Write Like the News* booklet, emphasizing journalistic clarity for policy communication.

### Lead With the Future
- Frame findings in terms of their forward implications where possible.
- "This trend suggests wages will remain below 2019 levels through 2026" > "Wages fell between 2019 and 2024."

### Be Positive and Direct
- State what is true, not what is not true.
- "Wages grew in 47 states" > "Wages did not decline in most states."

### Strong, Specific Verbs
- Use precise, active verbs.
- "surged," "contracted," "outpaced," "concentrated" > "went up," "went down," "was higher," "was found"
- Avoid weak nominalization: "The decline of wages" → "Wages declined."

### Short Paragraphs
- Body paragraphs: 2–4 sentences maximum in blog/brief format.
- One idea per paragraph.

### Transitions
- Transitions should signal the logical relationship (contrast: "Yet," "However"; addition: "Moreover," "Also"; causation: "As a result," "Therefore").
- Do not use "Additionally" as a filler transition.

## 10. AP-Style Numbers Addendum

EIG follows AP style as the baseline. Key AP rules:

- Spell out numbers one through nine; use figures for 10 and up.
- Always use figures for: ages, percentages, monetary amounts, times, dates, measurements.
- "Ages" take figures even under 10: "a 5-year-old child."
- Time: "3 p.m.," "10:30 a.m." (not "3 PM" or "10:30AM").
- Dates: "Jan. 5, 2023" (AP abbreviates months with six or more letters). Do not use ordinals with dates ("January 5th" → "January 5").
- Phone numbers: Use hyphens, not periods (202-555-0100).
- Large numbers: "1,234" (comma for thousands separator in U.S. style).

## Quick-Reference Checklist

Before submitting any EIG document, verify:

- [ ] Numbers <10 spelled out in text; % written as "percent" in text
- [ ] Oxford comma in all lists
- [ ] No spaces around em dashes
- [ ] Footnote markers outside punctuation
- [ ] "COVID-19" (caps, hyphen)
- [ ] "homeownership" (one word)
- [ ] "U.S." (with periods) as adjective; "United States" as noun
- [ ] No contractions
- [ ] Figure labels: "Figure N." + sentence-case caption
- [ ] Source line under every figure
- [ ] One term per concept (no elegant variation)
- [ ] Active voice; strong verbs
- [ ] Lede is ≤1 sentence and leads with what's new
