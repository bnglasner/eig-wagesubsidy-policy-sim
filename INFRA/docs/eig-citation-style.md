# EIG Citation and Notes Style Guide

Reference for formatting citations in EIG footnotes, endnotes, and bibliographies, and for writing library notes files. All EIG publications use a consistent footnote-style citation system derived from Chicago Notes with EIG-specific modifications.

---

## 1. General Rules

- **End with a period.** Every citation ends with a period, including those ending with a URL.
- **No parentheses around dates.** Year appears after author name(s), separated by a comma — not in parentheses.
- **Hyperlink the title** where the work is available online. The linked text is the title of the work (article, report, webpage), not a bare URL.
- **Commas between most elements** (author, year, title, publication, URL). See format-specific rules below.
- **No "Retrieved from" language.** Just include the URL or hyperlinked title.
- **Author format:** Last name, First name for the first author; subsequent authors: First Last. For organizations as authors, spell out the full name.
- **Multiple authors:** List all authors up to three. For four or more, list first author then "et al."
- **No "Ibid."** in EIG publications — repeat the citation in shortened form on second reference: Author Last Name, "Short Title," year.
- **Date of access:** Include for webpages only, in the format "accessed Month Day, Year" at the end of the citation.

---

## 2. Citation Formats by Type

### Article / Journal Article

```
Last, First, "Article Title," Journal Name, Volume (Issue), Year, pages. URL.
```

**Example:**
> Autor, David, David Dorn, and Gordon Hanson, "The China Syndrome: Local Labor Market Effects of Import Competition in the United States," *American Economic Review*, 103 (6), 2013, 2121–2168. https://doi.org/10.1257/aer.103.6.2121.

### Webpage / Online Article / Blog Post

```
Last, First, "Page or Post Title," Organization or Website Name, Month Day, Year. URL (accessed Month Day, Year).
```

**Example:**
> Lettieri, John, "The Geography of Opportunity," Economic Innovation Group, March 14, 2022. https://eig.org/geography-of-opportunity (accessed February 1, 2024).

### Report / Working Paper

```
Last, First, Report Title, Organization, Month Year. URL.
```

*Note: Report titles are not in quotation marks — they are italicized in formatted documents.*

**Example:**
> Glaeser, Edward L., and Joshua D. Gottlieb, *The Wealth of Cities: Agglomeration Economies and Spatial Equilibrium in the United States*, NBER Working Paper No. 15024, National Bureau of Economic Research, June 2009. https://www.nber.org/papers/w15024.

### Book

```
Last, First, Book Title, Publisher, Year.
```

**Example:**
> Case, Anne, and Angus Deaton, *Deaths of Despair and the Future of Capitalism*, Princeton University Press, 2020.

### Chapter in Edited Book

```
Last, First, "Chapter Title," in Editor First Last, ed., Book Title, Publisher, Year, pages.
```

**Example:**
> Moretti, Enrico, "Local Labor Markets," in Orley Ashenfelter and David Card, eds., *Handbook of Labor Economics*, Vol. 4B, Elsevier, 2011, 1237–1313.

### Law / Statute / Regulation

```
Official Name of Act, Public Law Number or U.S. Code Citation, Year.
```

**Example:**
> Opportunity Zones Provision, Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017, Pub. L. No. 115-97, § 13823, 131 Stat. 2054, 2183 (2017).

For regulations:
> Qualified Opportunity Zone Business Property, 26 C.F.R. § 1.1400Z2(d)-1 (2019).

### Podcast

```
Last, First, host, "Episode Title," Podcast Name, Month Day, Year. URL.
```

**Example:**
> Ryssdal, Kai, host, "Why Cities Are at a Crossroads," *Marketplace*, American Public Media, April 5, 2023. https://www.marketplace.org/episode/cities-crossroads.

### Press Release

```
Organization Name, "Press Release Title," Month Day, Year. URL.
```

**Example:**
> Economic Innovation Group, "EIG Releases New Distressed Communities Index," February 7, 2023. https://eig.org/news/eig-releases-new-distressed-communities-index.

### Data / Dataset

```
Organization Name, Dataset Name, Version (if applicable), Year. URL.
```

**Example:**
> U.S. Census Bureau, American Community Survey 5-Year Estimates, 2017–2021. https://data.census.gov.

## 3. In-Text Reference Style

EIG publications use **footnotes** (not in-text author-date citations). In running text:
- Do not include author names or years in parentheses.
- Place the footnote superscript **after** the closing punctuation of the sentence.
- On first reference, use a full citation. On subsequent references in the same document, use shortened form: Author Last Name, "Short Title," Year.

## 4. Library Notes Format

When adding a source to the EIG research library, create a notes file alongside the saved PDF. The file captures enough information to reconstruct the citation and assess relevance without re-reading the source.

### Filename Convention

```
YYYY-MM_LastnameFirstinitial_ShortTitle.md
```

**Examples:**
- `2023-04_AutorD_LaborMarketPolarization.md`
- `2022-09_CensusBureau_ACS5Year2017-2021.md`
- `2021-11_EIG_DistressedCommunitiesIndex.md`

### Notes File Template

```markdown
# [Full Title]

**Citation:**
[Full formatted citation in EIG style]

**Abstract / Summary:**
[2–4 sentence summary of the source's main argument, data, or findings. Write in your own words.]

**Key Points:**
- [Most relevant finding or data point for EIG's research]
- [Second most relevant point]
- [Additional points as needed]

**Data / Methodology Notes:**
[Any notes on data sources used, sample, geography, time period, or methodology caveats]

**EIG Relevance:**
[1–2 sentences on how this source may be useful for EIG projects — which topics, reports, or arguments it supports]

**Tags:**
[comma-separated keywords, e.g., wages, geographic inequality, Opportunity Zones, rural, small business]
```

### Library Notes Example

```markdown
# The China Syndrome: Local Labor Market Effects of Import Competition in the United States

**Citation:**
Autor, David, David Dorn, and Gordon Hanson, "The China Syndrome: Local Labor Market Effects of Import Competition in the United States," *American Economic Review*, 103 (6), 2013, 2121–2168. https://doi.org/10.1257/aer.103.6.2121.

**Abstract / Summary:**
Autor, Dorn, and Hanson estimate the effect of rising Chinese import competition on U.S. local labor markets using a shift-share instrument. Rising exposure to Chinese imports reduces manufacturing employment, earnings, and labor force participation in affected commuting zones, with limited offsetting gains in other sectors.

**Key Points:**
- A $1,000 increase in Chinese import exposure per worker reduces manufacturing employment by 0.6 percentage points.
- Affected workers experience lower earnings, higher unemployment, and greater reliance on disability and income transfer programs.
- Effects are concentrated in regions that already had a heavy manufacturing base.

**Data / Methodology Notes:**
Uses commuting zone-level panel data, 1990–2007. Instruments Chinese import growth to the U.S. with Chinese imports to other high-income countries to address endogeneity.

**EIG Relevance:**
Foundational source for any EIG work on geographic inequality, place-based economic distress, or trade's role in regional divergence. Useful for DCI context and manufacturing decline narratives.

**Tags:**
trade, manufacturing, geographic inequality, labor markets, commuting zones, import competition, China
```

## 5. Quick-Reference Checklist

Before submitting citations:

- [ ] Every citation ends with a period
- [ ] No parentheses around the year
- [ ] Title of article/post is in quotation marks; title of book/report is italicized
- [ ] URL is hyperlinked to the title (in digital documents)
- [ ] Date of access included for webpages
- [ ] Four or more authors → first author + "et al."
- [ ] Footnote marker is outside the closing punctuation
- [ ] Library notes filename follows YYYY-MM_LastnameFirstinitial_ShortTitle.md convention
